Background

Grantmaker. Researcher.
Field Builder.

I am a strategic grantmaker and applied researcher with a decade of experience at the intersection of gender equity, financial inclusion, education, and global development. My career spans multilateral institutions, philanthropy, and civil society — from the United Nations Capital Development Fund and the World Bank's Africa Gender Innovation Lab to Give Girls A Chance, the literacy nonprofit I co-founded.

My work is grounded in a conviction that durable change requires investing not just in programs, but in the people and systems that carry them. I design and steward grant portfolios with this in mind: rigorous due diligence, honest partner relationships, and a constant eye on whether the work is doing what we said it would do.

My graduate training in medical physics and medical informatics gives me scientific fluency that is unusual in philanthropy — I can read technical evidence critically, assess study design, and evaluate claims made by grantees working in health, behavioral science, and data-driven fields.

I am based in Washington, DC. I write, research, present, and mentor. And I co-founded a nonprofit that has gotten more than 1,000 girls in Nigeria to pick up a pen and think hard about the world.

Impact Highlights

World Bank · Africa Gender Innovation Lab

Ethiopia Gender Scorecard

Co-led development of a first-of-its-kind tool assessing how financial institutions serve women at the institutional and product level. Ethiopia became only the second country globally to adopt such a framework. Findings were incorporated into Ethiopia's National Financial Inclusion Strategy and shaped central bank policy targets.

Civil Society Work

Give Girls A Chance

Give Girls A Chance (GGAC) is a nonprofit I co-founded to advance literacy, education, and economic empowerment for girls and young women in underserved communities in Nigeria. It is also the most personal expression of what I believe about how durable change happens.

The centerpiece is an annual national essay competition — now in its 8th year — designed to build reading comprehension, critical thinking, and sustained writing practice among primary and secondary school girls. But the competition is only one piece. GGAC operates as a whole-child literacy ecosystem, engaging families, teachers, and communities alongside students, because we know that literacy improvement requires ecosystem engagement to be lasting.

To date, GGAC has mobilized more than $3M in philanthropic funding, partnered with 150+ schools across 20 states, and tracked measurable performance gains among girls enrolled in our programs compared to non-participating peers.
